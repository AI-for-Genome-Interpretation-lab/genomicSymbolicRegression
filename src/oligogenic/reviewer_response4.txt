We thank the reviewer for this suggestion. We have extended our benchmarking in two directions.

First, we have added PLINK2 epistasis as a representative second-order, interpretable baseline. PLINK2 computes pairwise association statistics between variants and the phenotype, providing a direct comparison to SR in terms of both predictive performance and the ability to identify interacting variant pairs. Results are reported alongside the existing baselines in the revised manuscript.

Second, regarding tree-based methods, we have included Random Forest in our benchmarking across all datasets (see also our response to Reviewer X, comment Y). In terms of classification performance (AUROC/AUPRC), Random Forest performs comparably to or slightly better than SR in most settings, confirming that tree-based models are strong competitors for predictive accuracy.

However, the core advantage of SR via Feyn over both PLINK2 and Random Forest lies in the nature of its output. PLINK2 is limited to pairwise interactions by design and does not generalise to higher-order combinations. Random Forest captures nonlinear interactions implicitly through an ensemble of hundreds of trees, but the resulting model is not directly interpretable. In contrast, Feyn returns a single explicit mathematical formula over the input variants — directly naming the interacting loci and quantifying their joint contribution — which can be inspected, reported, and communicated to clinicians or biologists without post-hoc explanation tools. In the context of epistasis detection, where the scientific goal is to identify which specific variant combinations drive the phenotype, this interpretability constitutes a distinct practical advantage that goes beyond raw predictive accuracy.
