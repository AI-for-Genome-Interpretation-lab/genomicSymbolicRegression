We thank the reviewer for raising this important point. We address scalability through our oligogenic disease simulation experiments, which represent a substantially more challenging and realistic genomic setting than the GB1 benchmark.

Specifically, the three OLIDA-based datasets span up to 7,529 variants (Alport syndrome) and up to 1,400 samples, with the feature space consisting of rare variants drawn from the 1000 Genomes Project across multiple genes on different chromosomes. To manage the search space at this scale, we applied a pre-filtering step that retains the top 2,500 variants by marginal correlation with the phenotype before passing the data to Feyn, reflecting a realistic two-stage analysis pipeline common in genetic association studies.

Under this regime, Feyn SR remains tractable and continues to return interpretable formulas identifying specific interacting variant combinations. Accuracy and runtime results for these larger datasets are reported in the Supplementary Material.

We acknowledge that datasets with hundreds of thousands of samples and SNPs — as encountered in biobank-scale GWAS — would require additional engineering, such as more aggressive pre-filtering, distributed computation, or integration with dimensionality reduction. We discuss these directions explicitly in the Limitations section of the revised manuscript, and note that the current implementation is primarily targeted at candidate-region or gene-panel analyses rather than genome-wide scans, where the variant space can be constrained to biologically motivated loci.
