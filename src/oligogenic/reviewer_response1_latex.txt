% ── Add to Results / Benchmarking section ─────────────────────────────────────

To broaden the comparison beyond linear models and neural networks, we
included Random Forest (500 trees) as a representative tree-based baseline
capable of capturing nonlinear and higher-order interactions.
Across all datasets and noise conditions, Random Forest achieves
classification performance (AUROC/AUPRC) comparable to or slightly above
that of SR-based models (see Supplementary Figure~\ref{fig:supp_rf_comparison}),
confirming that ensemble tree methods are strong predictive competitors.

Despite this parity in accuracy, SR via Feyn offers a qualitatively
different output: rather than an ensemble of hundreds of trees whose joint
decision boundary is not directly interpretable, Feyn returns a single
explicit mathematical formula over the input variants, for example:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{y} = \sigma\!\left(w_1\, v_A + w_2\, v_B + w_3\, v_A v_B + b\right)
    \label{eq:feyn_example}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $v_A$ and $v_B$ are dosage values at two specific loci,
$w_1, w_2, w_3$ are learned coefficients, and $\sigma$ is the logistic
function. This formula directly names the interacting variants and
quantifies their joint contribution, making it immediately interpretable
without post-hoc explanation tools.
In the context of oligogenic disease, where the scientific goal is to
identify \emph{which} variant combinations drive disease risk, such an
explicit representation is more actionable than a feature importance
ranking derived from a Random Forest
(Supplementary Table~\ref{tab:supp_jaccard_rf}).
